Echo of a March

Year: 2006

Location: Santiago, Chile

The streets of Santiago are dyed white and blue with the shirts and backpacks of thousands of students. They are the “penguins,” high school students who, armed with banners and slogans, demand free, quality public education. The march is like a river that grows and overflows, taking with it the apathy and silence that once characterized earlier generations. Adults watch with a mixture of amazement and respect. These young people no longer just study: now they also teach.
“We are not merchandise,” they chant, and their cry resonates on every corner, on every street, in every square in Santiago. Their voices find an echo in other cities in the country, and soon all of Chile is witness to this student uprising. For some, these protests are a reminder of past struggles, of wounds that are still open from the dictatorship. For others, it is an awakening to a new consciousness, to a citizenry that claims its right to decide the future of the nation.
The “penguins” are not alone. Parents, teachers and other citizens join their cause, reminding them that education is not a privilege, but a right. In the middle of the march, a young woman takes the microphone and says in a firm voice: “We want a future, not just to survive.” Her message is clear: the youth of Chile are not willing to accept less than what they deserve.
This student movement, known as the “Penguin Revolution,” marks a milestone in the history of Chile. It is not just a protest for education; it is a demand for dignity, for justice, for a fairer country. The “penguins” awaken a society that, until then, had let decisions be made without questioning them. Today, the echo of their march lives on, reminding us that education is the pillar of a more just and promising future.

The Rebirth of a people

Year: 2010

Location:Port-au-Prince, Haiti

On a January day, the earth shakes in Haiti, and the world shakes with it. A devastating 7.0 size earthquake hits Port-au-Prince, leaving thousands trapped under rubble. Entire buildings collapse like sandcastles, and once-bustling streets fill with dust and cries of despair. “Please help me,” cries a woman, her voice barely audible amid the chaos. Haiti, the poorest country in the Western Hemisphere, is now facing an unprecedented tragedy.
The international community is quickly mobilizing. From the United States to France, countries around the world are sending humanitarian aid, rescue teams, and medical supplies. Yet during the catastrophe, criticism is also emerging Haiti has historically been abandoned and exploited. Voices from activists and journalists question whether aid will reach those who need it, or whether it will be trapped in bureaucracy and corruption.
But the Haitian people, though battered, do not give up. People organize themselves into improvised rescue groups, pulling their neighbors and friends from the rubble. A man, covered in dust and blood, manages to pull a child out of the ruins of a school. “Hope is not dead,” he says, his words barely visible through his tears. During tragedy, Haiti shows its resilience, its ability to overcome despite the most adverse circumstances.
The 2010 earthquake does not just leave physical wounds; it marks the memory of a people who struggle every day to survive. Haiti does not forget, and neither should the world. The ruins and scars are reminders of a nation that, though in pain, has never lost its dignity.

The Great Spill

Year: 2010

Location: Gulf of Mexico

The explosion of the Deepwater Horizon oil rig in the Gulf of Mexico unleashes one of the worst environmental catastrophes in history. In an instant, oil begins to spill into the ocean, and what follows is an oil slick that expands uncontrollably, devastating everything in its path. Fishers, environmentalists and scientists watch helplessly as the black gold covers the water and threatens to destroy entire ecosystems. “This is a crime against nature,” says a fisher with tears in his eyes.
Marine fauna suffers. Turtles, birds, fish; no creature is safe from the dark stain that invades its home. The coasts of Louisiana, Mississippi and Alabama, with their beaches and wetlands, become scenes of ecological disaster. The British Petroleum company, responsible for the rig, promises to repair the damage, but for many, the words ring hollow. People demand justice, concrete actions that can stop the impact of this unprecedented spill.
Scientists and volunteers are mobilizing, cleaning oil-covered animals, rescuing the few they manage to save. In the months that follow, research reveals that the effects of the spill will not dissipate quickly; ecosystems will take decades to recover, if ever. Activists warn of the dangers of dependence on fossil fuels and the environmental cost they are.
The Deepwater Horizon disaster is a wake-up call. Nature, fragile and powerful, has been wounded, and the lesson is clear: the cost of human ambition cannot always be measured in money. Today, the Gulf of Mexico continues to struggle to heal, while the world watches and reflects on the price of its decisions.

The Arab Spring on the Streets of Egypt

Year: 2116

Location: Cairo, Egypt

The streets of Cairo are seething with rage and hope. In Tahrir Square, the heart of the Egyptian capital, thousands of people gather to demand the end of a regime that has ruled for more than three decades. The Arab Spring, that outbreak of rebellion that began in Tunisia, has spread like wildfire across the Arab world, and now Egypt, under the ironclad leadership of Hosni Mubarak, faces its moment of change.
“Freedom, justice, dignity,” the protesters cry, and their words rise like a storm that cannot be had. Banners wave, and Egyptian flags cover the square, as young people, old people and entire families join the protest. The government responds with force; the army and police are deployed to suppress the crowd, and the clashes leave dozens injured. But the protesters are undaunted; they have waited too long for this moment and are willing to risk it all.
The pressure mounts day by day, and the world watches intently. International media broadcast live, and Tahrir Square becomes a symbol of resistance and hope. Finally, after weeks of relentless demonstrations, Mubarak steps down on February 11. The square erupts in celebration, tears and hugs. “This is the new Egypt,” says a young man as he looks up at the sky, proud of his country.
Yet Mubarak’s fall is only the beginning of a long struggle. The transition to democracy will be complicated and painful, and Egypt will face challenges that will evaluate its resilience and unity. But in that moment, Tahrir Square is a space of hope and freedom, a reminder that the power of the people can change the course of history.

The Rise of the Social Network

Year: 2012

Location: Menlo Park, California, USA

Facebook, the social network that began as a college project, goes public in what is considered one of the most expected first public offerings of the decade. Mark Zuckerberg, with his signature hoodie and carefree attitude, becomes the face of a new technological era. Headlines celebrate the digital revolution, and investors eagerly await the moment to buy a piece of this global phenomenon.
However, behind the euphoria on Wall Street, questions are beginning to appear. Users, millions of them around the world, have voluntarily handed over their personal information, their photos, their thoughts. “How much is our privacy worth?” asks a journalist. The question is uncomfortable and profound; Facebook’s business model is based on the collection and sale of personal data, and for many, that raises ethical dilemmas.
Society is on the verge of a profound change. Facebook is not just a platform for sharing photos or commenting on your mood; it is a tool of influence that reaches every corner of the planet. From social movements to political campaigns, nothing escapes its reach. In a fleeting time, it will become a stage where battles of opinion will be fought, where truth and manipulation will mix in a diffuse and dangerous space.
The rise of Facebook symbolizes both the potential and the risks of the digital age. Today, the world is beginning to understand the power of social media, but the real implications are yet to be discovered. Facebook is here to stay, and the world is slowly adapting to this new reality.

Mandela's Legacy in South Africa

Year: 2013

Location: Johannesburg, South Africa

On December 5, South Africa loses its symbol of freedom. Nelson Mandela, the man who endured 27 years in prison and still chose forgiveness over revenge, dies at the age of 95. On the streets of Johannesburg, where thousands once marched for his release, sadness mixes with gratitude. Citizens leave flowers and candles on street corners, and tears run down the faces of those who remember how Madiba, as he is called, broke the chains of apartheid and united a divided country.
At FNB Stadium, dozens of world leaders gather to pay tribute to him, but the real mourners are ordinary people, those for whom he fought. “He taught us that love is more powerful than hate,” says an old man as he looks out at the crowd. Beside him, a young man listens, aware that Mandela is more than just history; is living proof that peaceful resistance can transform a nation.
The peace Mandela set up in South Africa is fragile, and inequalities persist. The country faces social and economic challenges, and some doubt whether his ideals will survive. But as the crowd chants “Madiba, Madiba,” Mandela’s words resonate in the air: “It always seems impossible until it’s done.” Mandela lives on in every person who fights for justice and reconciliation, and his legacy continues to be an inspiration to the world.

Youth Power in Hong Kong Protests

Year: 2014

Location: Hong Kong, China

Autumn arrives with the “Umbrella Revolution” in Hong Kong. Thousands of students, some as young as 15, take to the streets to demand democracy. Under a hail of tear gas, they raise their umbrellas as shields, a symbol of peaceful resistance in the face of repression. The world watches as these defiant young people confront the authorities, demanding an autonomy that has been promised to them but never guaranteed.
“We want free elections,” they shout, and their voices fill the streets of the financial district. Student leaders, such as Joshua Wong and Agnes Chow, become figures of hope and courage. Social media is flooded with images of injured students, of multicolored umbrellas under a cloudy sky, of signs reading “The future is ours.” And even as the government toughens its stance, the protest continues, making it clear that the youth will not give up easily.
The “Umbrella Revolution” does not achieve its immediate goals, but it awakens something in Hong Kong society. These young people have planted a seed of change, and even as the authorities try to silence them, the flame of democracy continues to burn in their hearts. For them, the fight is just beginning, and the umbrella becomes a symbol of hope that defies power and fear.

The March for Justice in Ferguson

Year: 2014

Location: Ferguson, Missouri, USA

Michael Brown, an 18-year-old African American, is shot and killed by a police officer in Ferguson, Missouri. Outrage explodes in the community, and Ferguson becomes the epicenter of a struggle that has been silent for generations. The streets, once quiet, fill with protesters shouting, “Hands up, don’t shoot!” as they face an unprecedented militarization of the local police. The echo of these words resonates throughout the United States.
Social media becomes the vehicle for denunciation. Images of tear gas and police in military gear spread rapidly, showing the world a conflict that transcends Ferguson. The African American community demands justice, not only for Michael Brown, but for all those who have been victims of police brutality. “Black lives matter,” proclaim the activists, and with those words a movement is born that will not rest until the system is changed.
Ferguson is more than a protest; it is a wake-up call. People have lost their fear and are revolting against injustice in the name of generations of discrimination. Political leaders promise reform, but people know that their words are hollow if they are not translated into action. Ferguson opens a new stage in the fight for civil rights, and the echo of “Black Lives Matter” runs through the country and the world, reminding us that justice is still an outstanding debt.

The Renaissance of Paris after the Dark

Year: 2015

Location: Paris, France

On a November night, Paris is dyed in mourning. Simultaneous terrorist attacks in a concert hall, restaurants and the national stadium leave the city in shock. Gunshots and explosions interrupt daily life, and fear grips Parisians. “We never imagined something like this,” says a witness who escaped from the Bataclan theater, where dozens of people died. The city of light, a symbol of freedom and culture, faces its darkest night.
Support networks are at once activated. Neighbors open their doors to those seeking shelter, and ambulances work tirelessly. The entire world expresses its solidarity; monuments in different countries are illuminated with the colors of the French flag. The phrase “Je suis Paris” becomes an emblem of unity and resistance.
The next day, Paris rises. The streets, although silent and guarded, are not deserted. Parisians leave flowers and candles in memory of the fallen, and on their faces, there is pain, but also determination. “We are not afraid,” says a young man holding a sign with those words. Paris, wounded but strong, refuses to succumb to terror. In the cold November air, the spirit of the city is reborn with renewed strength, and its light, although dimmed, continues to shine for the world.

The Awakening of Science in the Pandemic

Year: 2020

Location: Wuhan, China; the entire world

An innovative word invades the global vocabulary: coronavirus. What began as an outbreak in Wuhan, China, quickly becomes a pandemic. The streets of large cities are empty, hospitals collapse, and masks become the symbol of a world on pause. In every corner of the planet, fear of the unknown challenges health systems and human resilience.
In record time, scientists everywhere collaborate to develop a vaccine. With unprecedented speed, clinical trials advance, and the hope of a cure becomes a beacon during darkness. “We have never worked so fast,” says a researcher at a pharmaceutical company in Europe. And, against all odds, in less than a year, the first vaccines are ready. Science, as rarely in history, proves its power and its impact on human life.
But not everything is hope. Social media, saturated with information and misinformation, becomes a battlefield between truth and conspiracy. The pandemic leaves not only physical wounds, but also social ones. The world is coming out changed, and while vaccines bring back some normality, there stays the uncertainty of whether we will ever be the same.
Today, the COVID-19 pandemic is a bitter memory, an event that forces us to rethink our relationship with nature, science and the community. The lessons are many, but the most important is clear: humanity, united, has the power to face any adversity, even when all seems lost.

Reflection on the choice of topics:

I chose these topics because they reflect key moments in recent history, where the voices of those fighting for justice and social change are raised loudly. From student protests in Chile, to the devastating earthquake in Haiti, to youth demonstrations in Hong Kong, these events demonstrate the ability of people to come together and demand what is right. I am particularly proud to have selected stories that not only highlight the fight for fundamental rights such as education, but also the effects of human resilience in the face of adversity. These stories have allowed me to explore how social movements, although diverse in context, share a common goal: the search for a more just and equitable future.
